Act I · The problem

Models don’t read words. They read tokens.

A language model can’t look at raw letters — it needs a fixed dictionary of pieces called tokens. A tokenizer chops text into those pieces. Too few tokens per word and the vocabulary explodes; too many and the model wastes effort. The sweet spot is a small vocabulary that still slices most words cleanly.

Our whole assignment lives in one number: the ratio = tokens ÷ words. A ratio of 1.0 means every word is a single token. The further above 1.0, the more a language gets chopped up.

💡 BPE was introduced for NLP by Sennrich, Haddow & Birch (2016) 🧩 GPT-2, GPT-4, LLaMA all tokenize with BPE variants 📦 Original idea: a 1994 data-compression algorithm by Philip Gage
Act II · The algorithm

How BPE learns: merge the most common pair, again and again

Start with single characters. Find the most frequent adjacent pair across the corpus, glue it into a new token, and repeat. Each merge adds one token to the vocabulary. Watch it happen on a tiny corpus:

Act III · Four languages, three scripts, one budget

The catch: the scripts don’t share letters

Merges only happen between characters that actually sit next to each other — so each writing system carves out its own slice of the 10,000-token budget. Give English more, and the Indic scripts starve.

LATIN · English
Aa
Short words, spaces between them, a big head of very common words. The easiest to push toward ratio 1.0.
DEVANAGARI · Hindi + Sanskrit
भा
Two languages, one script. Hindi and Sanskrit pool the same merges — a rare bonus. Sanskrit’s long compound words are hard, but they ride on Hindi’s syllables.
TELUGU · on its own
తె
An isolated script with highly inflected, rarely-repeating words — and the smallest budget share. The hardest to compress.
✍️ Devanagari also writes Marathi, Nepali & more 🔤 Telugu is an abugida — consonants carry built-in vowels 🕉️ Sanskrit “sandhi” fuses words together, inflating length

Method. Text extracted from the four uploaded Wikipedia PDFs, NFC-normalised (duplicated Indic matra artifacts collapsed), stripped of citation markers, URLs and reference sections. One word-level BPE tokenizer (Whitespace pre-tokeniser, shared 10,000-token vocab) trained on all four languages.
